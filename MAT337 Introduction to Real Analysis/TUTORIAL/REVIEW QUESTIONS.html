Question Using Taylor expansion for $\frac{\sin x}{x}$, find $\int_0^1 \frac{\sin x}{x} dx$

Consider $\sin(x) = \sum \frac{(-1)^n x^{2k+1}}{(2k+1)!}\Rightarrow \frac{\sin(x)}{x} = \sum \frac{(-1)^n x^{2k}}{(2k+1)!}:=S(x)$
Using Hadamard's Theorem, $\frac{a_{k+1}}{a_k} = \frac{(2k+1)!}{(2k+3)!} = (2k+1)^{-1}(2k+2)^{-1}\rightarrow 0\Rightarrow R = \infty$
$x^2$ has infinite radius of convergence $\Rightarrow x\in\mathbb R$

Therefore, $\forall x\in \mathbb R. \sin x/x \rightarrow^{u.c.} S(x)$, hence ICT is valid \begin{align*} \int_0^1 \sin x/x dx &= \int_0^1 \sum \frac{(-1)^k x^{2k}}{(2k+1)!}dx \\ &= \lim_{N} \sum^N \int_0^1 \frac{(-1)^k x^{2k}}{(2k+1)!}dx &\text{linearity of sum and ICT} \\ &= \lim_{N} \sum^N \frac{(-1)^k}{(2k+1)!(2k+1)} < \infty \end{align*}

Question Show $\mathcal F = \{e^{-ax^2}: a\in [0,1]\}$ is compact in $(C[-1,1], \|\cdot\|_\infty)$

Bounded: Known that $e^x$ is monotone increasing, since $a \geq 0, x^2 \geq 0, -ax^2 \leq 0\Rightarrow e^{-ax^2} < e^0 = 1$, and note $\forall x\in\mathbb R. e^x \geq 0\Rightarrow \mathcal F$ is bounded by $[0,1]$.

Equicontinuous: Let $\epsilon > 0$, take $\delta = \epsilon/2$. Let $a \in [0,1]$. Let $x,y\in[-1,1], x-y < \delta$ (wlog).
\begin{align*} |e^{-ax^2} - e^{-ay^2}| &= |-2a\xi e^{-a\xi^2}(x-y)| &\xi\in(x,y)\text{, by MVT}\\ &\leq 2a\xi\delta &e^{-a\xi^2}\leq 1\\ &\leq 2\delta &\xi\in(x,y)\Rightarrow \xi \leq 1\\ &=\epsilon \end{align*}

Closed: Suppose not, hence exists $f_n\rightarrow f\not\in \mathcal F$. Consider such $f_n := e^{-a_nx^2}$. However, $[0,1]$ compact, hence $a_n\rightarrow a\in [0,1]$. And $\forall x_0\in [-1,1]$ \begin{align*} |e^{-a_nx_0^2} - e^{-ax^2_0}| &= |x^2_0e^{-c}(a_n-a)| &\text{MVT respect to }a \\ &\leq |a_n - a| \\ \Rightarrow \|e^{-a_nx^2} - e^{-ax^2}\|&\rightarrow 0 \end{align*} Then, $e^{-a_nx^2}\rightarrow^{u.c.}e^{-ax^2}$, and since $a\in[0,1]$, it is closed.

Question Find the closure of $p_n(x^2,y^2)\in C([-1,1]^2)$

Consider the limit points of some $p_n$, i.e. $p_n\rightarrow^{u.c.}f$. Then,
