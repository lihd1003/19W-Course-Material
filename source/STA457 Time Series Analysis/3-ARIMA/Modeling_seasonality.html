Commonly used time series models for seasonality

1. STL:

The Seasonal-Trend Decomposition Procedure Based on Loess (STL) of Cleveland et al. (1990) is widely used by statisticans and econometricians to capture the trend and seasonal components in time series.

2. SARIMA

The Box-Jenkins approach is one of the most popular methods for studying time series. This approach formulates seasonal time series as Seasonal Autoregressive and Moving Average model (SARIMA) models, which rely on seasonal differencing to take into account seasonal effects. Do not confuse with Periodic time series models, which considers different parameterization of ARMA models at different periods.

3. Structural time series models

The structural time series model formulates time series directly in terms of the traditional components of trend, seasonal, cycle and irregular. The attraction of such a formulation is that it has an immediate interpretation and so is a natural vehicle for making forecasts. Moreover, it is easy to incorporate explanatory variables of interest into a structural time series model and estimate it using the Kalman filter. Therefore, the use of structural time series models has become a main stream in morden econometrics and widely used in forecasting macroeconomic variables.

4. Holt-Winters exponential smoothing:

The Holt-Winters exponential smoothing method has been proposed for more than 50 years and still been used in many business forecasting exercises.

1. Simple exponential smoothing:

Let \(Y_t(1)\) denotes the one-step ahead forecast at time \(t\). Brown (1959) suggests a forecast scheme based on the forecast for the previous period and adjusting it using the forecast errors. That is, \[ Y_t(1)=Y_{t-1}(1)+\alpha (Y_t-Y_{t-1}(1)),\] where \(0<\alpha<1\). We can also express the above equation as \[ Y_t(1)=\alpha Y_t + (1-\alpha) Y_{t-1}(1).\] The forecast \(Y_t(1)\) is based on weighting the most recent observation \(Y_t\) with a weight value \(\alpha\) and weighting the most recent forecast \(Y_{t-1}(1)\) with a weight of \(1-\alpha\).

2. Holt linear method:

  1. Level: \[ l_t = \alpha Y_t + (1-\alpha) (l_{t-1}+b_{t-1}) \]
  2. Trend: \[ b_t = \beta (l_t-l_{t-1}) +(1-\beta) b_{t-1} \]
  3. Forecast: \[ y_t(h)=l_t+b_t h\]

2. Holt-Winters’ linear and seasonality method:

  1. Level: \[ l_t = \alpha (Y_t-Y_{t-m}) + (1-\alpha) (l_{t-1}+b_{t-1}) \]
  2. Trend: \[ b_t = \beta (l_t-l_{t-1}) +(1-\beta) b_{t-1} \]
  3. Seasonal: \[s_t = \gamma (Y_t-l_{t-1}-b_{t-1})+(1-\gamma) s_{t-m}\]
  4. Forecast: \[ y_t(h)=l_t+b_t h+s_{t-m+h}\]
Remark: Hyndman (2002) provides a good summary of various exponential smoothing models and their connection with state-space models.

1. Data: Monthly Airline Passenger Numbers 1949-1960

head(AirPassengers)   
## [1] 112 118 132 129 121 135
par(mfrow=c(1,2))
plot(AirPassengers,type="o", main="Monthly Airline Passenger Numbers 1949-1960")
plot(log10(AirPassengers),type="o", main="Monthly Airline Passenger Numbers 1949-1960")

air<-ts(log10(AirPassengers),start=c(1949,1),freq=12)
monthplot(air,main="Monthly plot of Monthly Airline Passenger Numbers", type="l")

2. STL

par(mfrow=c(1,2))
plot(stl(air,s.window=35))

par(mfrow=c(4,3),mar=c(2,4,1,2),cex=0.6)
for(mon in month.abb) {
        plot(subset(stl(air,s.window=35)$time.series[,1],month =mon),type="o",main=mon,ylab=NA)
        abline(h=0,col=2,lty="dashed")
}

plot(forecast(stl(air,s.window=35)))
